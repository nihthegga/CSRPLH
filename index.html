CSRP law handbook

accurate to california law; grouped by category; each entry: statute, definition, real-world examples, exclusions, and penalties. this is practical information, not legal advice. However everything was fact checked, verify against cited code sections before relying on ingame.

defs PC §15 crime definition; PC §17 felony/misdemeanor/infraction.
felonies use statutory triads; many are wobblers (can be misdemeanor or felony). infractions are non-criminal fines.
crimes against persons property & habitation sexual offenses public peace & justice weapons & firearms drugs (health & safety) vehicle code (driving) fraud & forgery other & common

definitions & classes

crime / public offense
PC §15
act or omission violating a law with a punishment attached (death, imprisonment, fine, removal from office, or disqualification).
examples that count:
• stealing property (theft)
• hitting someone (battery)
• driving under influence
what doesn't count:
• civil contract violations without criminal penalties • parking violations (administrative) • school disciplinary rules
classes
PC §17
felony: punishable by death, state prison, or county jail under 1170(h). misdemeanor: other crimes not infractions; up to 1 year jail unless stated. infraction: non-criminal, fine only.
examples by class:
• felonies: murder, robbery, burglary, grand theft
• misdemeanors: simple assault, petty theft, DUI (1st offense)
• infractions: speeding tickets, parking violations, seatbelt violations
• wobblers: assault with deadly weapon, forgery, some drug possession

crimes against persons

murder (first degree)
PC §187, §189–§190
unlawful killing with malice; first degree includes willful/deliberate/premeditated, certain felony-murder.
examples that count:
• planning and carrying out a shooting
• killing during a robbery, burglary, rape, or kidnapping
• using poison or explosive device
• killing police officer in line of duty
• lying in wait to ambush someone
what doesn't count:
• killing in lawful self-defense • accidental death without malice • heat of passion killing (voluntary manslaughter instead)
penalty:
25-to-life; special circumstances: LWOP or death (moratorium in effect, statute remains).
murder (second degree)
PC §187, §190
malicious killing not meeting first-degree criteria.
examples that count:
• shooting someone in anger without premeditation
• killing during a fight with malicious intent
• reckless conduct showing implied malice (firing into crowd)
penalty:
15-to-life (higher if peace officer victim under specified facts).
manslaughter — voluntary
PC §192(a)
killing upon sudden quarrel or heat of passion.
examples that count:
• finding spouse in bed with another and killing in rage
• bar fight escalating to fatal violence
• road rage incident resulting in death
penalty:
3/6/11 years state prison.
kidnapping
PC §207
forcible movement/asportation of a person without consent.
examples that count:
• forcing someone into a car at gunpoint
• tying someone up and moving them to another location
• tricking child into car and driving away
• holding ex-partner against their will in basement
what doesn't count:
• brief movement during another crime (robbery) • consensual movement • parental custody of own minor child (usually)
penalty:
typically 3/5/8 years; aggravated forms higher.
robbery — first degree
PC §211; degrees §212.5; penalties §213
taking property from person/immediate presence by force or fear; first degree in inhabited dwelling, driver/passenger of transit, or ATM scenarios.
examples that count:
• home invasion robbery while family is present
• robbing taxi or uber driver
• mugging someone at an ATM
• robbing bus passengers
what doesn't count:
• stealing after victim already left (theft, not robbery) • taking property without force/fear • shoplifting without confrontation
penalty:
3/4/6 years; 3/6/9 if in inhabited structure with 2+ accomplices.
robbery — second degree
PC §211, §212.5(c), §213(a)(2)
all robberies not in first-degree category.
examples that count:
• mugging someone on street
• robbing store clerk at gunpoint
• purse snatching with force/struggle
• threatening store customer to take their wallet
penalty:
2/3/5 years.
assault (simple)
PC §240
unlawful attempt with present ability to commit violent injury.
examples that count:
• swinging fist at someone (missing or connecting)
• throwing object at person within range
• threatening with raised fist when close enough to hit
what doesn't count:
• verbal threats alone without physical ability • accidental contact • threatening from too far away to actually hurt someone
penalty:
misdemeanor; up to 6 months county jail and/or fine (~$1,000).
battery (simple)
PC §242
willful and unlawful use of force or violence upon another.
examples that count:
• punching, slapping, or kicking someone
• spitting on another person
• pushing someone during argument
• throwing drink on someone
penalty:
misdemeanor; up to 6 months jail and/or up to ~$2,000 fine. enhanced if injury/protected victim.
assault with a deadly weapon (adw)
PC §245(a)(1)
assault with deadly weapon/instrument other than a firearm, or force likely to cause great bodily injury.
examples that count:
• attacking with knife, bat, or hammer
• hitting with car (vehicle as weapon)
• throwing heavy objects like bricks
• attacking with broken bottle
• kicking someone in head while wearing boots
penalty:
wobbler; felony 2/3/4 years state prison or up to 1 year county; fines possible.

property & habitation

burglary — first degree (residential)
PC §459; degrees §460; penalties §461
entry of an inhabited dwelling with intent to commit theft or any felony.
examples that count:
• breaking into house to steal electronics
• entering apartment through unlocked door to commit rape
• climbing through window of inhabited mobile home
• entering hotel room to steal guest's belongings
what doesn't count:
• entering without criminal intent, then deciding to steal • breaking into abandoned house • entering open-air areas not enclosed structures
penalty:
2/4/6 years (state prison).
burglary — second degree (commercial/other)
PC §459–§461
burglary of any non-residence structure.
examples that count:
• breaking into store after hours to steal merchandise
• entering office building to steal computers
• breaking into warehouse or storage unit
• entering someone's garage or shed to steal tools
penalty:
wobbler; felony 16 months/2/3 years county; misdemeanor up to 1 year; fines possible.
theft — petty
PC §484, §488
theft of property with value generally < $950 (subject to changes & special rules).
examples that count:
• stealing clothes worth $300 from store
• taking someone's bike valued at $400
• pocketing $200 cash from unlocked car
• stealing groceries worth $50
penalty:
usually misdemeanor; up to 6 months jail/fine; priors/props can alter.
theft — grand
PC §487
theft meeting value thresholds or specific property categories.
examples that count:
• stealing jewelry worth $2,000
• taking someone's car (regardless of value)
• stealing livestock (horses, cattle)
• theft of firearm (any value)
• stealing $500+ from person directly
penalty:
wobbler; typical felony triad 16/24/36 months unless specified.
shoplifting (during business hours)
PC §459.5
entering commercial establishment during regular hours with intent to commit larceny where value ≤ $950.
examples that count:
• concealing items in purse at department store
• switching price tags to pay less
• eating food in grocery store without paying
• walking out with merchandise hidden in clothing
penalty:
generally misdemeanor; exceptions for serious/violent priors. repeat-theft rules may enhance under later measures.
vandalism
PC §594
maliciously defacing/damaging/destroying property not your own.
examples that count:
• spray painting graffiti on building walls
• keying someone's car
• breaking windows out of anger
• carving initials into park bench
penalty:
wobbler depending on damage; fines, jail/prison possible.
receiving stolen property
PC §496
buying/receiving/concealing/selling property knowing it to be stolen.
examples that count:
• buying obviously stolen electronics from stranger
• accepting "hot" car parts knowing they're stolen
• helping friend hide stolen merchandise
• purchasing expensive items well below market value suspiciously
penalty:
wobbler; value and priors affect charge.

sexual offenses

rape
PC §261
non-consensual sexual intercourse by force/violence/duress/menace or where consent is legally invalid (e.g., intoxication, unconscious).
examples that count:
• sexual intercourse using physical force
• intercourse with unconscious person
• intercourse with severely intoxicated person unable to consent
• using threats of harm to coerce intercourse
penalty:
felony; years vary with circumstances and enhancements.
lewd acts with a minor
PC §288
lewd or lascivious acts with a child under 14 (or specified ages with aggravators).
examples that count:
• inappropriate touching of child's private areas
• having child touch adult inappropriately
• sexual conduct with child under 14
penalty:
felony; lengthy state prison terms; registration requirements.
indecent exposure
PC §314
willfully and lewdly exposing private parts where others are present and offended.
examples that count:
• exposing genitals in public park
• "flashing" strangers on street
• masturbating in view of others
what doesn't count:
• breastfeeding in public • accidental exposure • nudity in appropriate settings (nude beaches, own home)
penalty:
misdemeanor/felony depending on priors/circumstances.

public peace & justice

disturbing the peace
PC §415
fighting in public; loud/unreasonable noise; offensive words likely to provoke immediate violence.
examples that count:
• loud party music at 3am disturbing neighbors
• fighting in public street
• yelling racial slurs to provoke fight
• shouting obscenities in crowded area
penalty:
misdemeanor/infraction depending facts.
criminal threats
PC §422
threatening death/gbh with specific intent it be taken as a threat, causing sustained fear; can be written, verbal, or electronic.
examples that count:
• texting "I'm going to kill you" to ex-partner
• leaving voicemail threatening to burn down someone's house
• email threatening to harm someone's family
• verbally threatening to shoot coworker
what doesn't count:
• vague threats without specifics • obvious jokes/hyperbole • threats that don't cause actual fear
penalty:
wobbler; up to felony years; strike in some cases.
false imprisonment
PC §236–§237
unlawful violation of personal liberty (restraining/detaining).
examples that count:
• locking someone in room against their will
• blocking car door to prevent someone leaving
• tying someone up during argument
• physically restraining person from leaving area
penalty:
misdemeanor or felony depending aggravators (violence/fraud/deceit).

weapons & firearms

firearm prohibitions / possession
PC §29800, §29805, §25400 (carry concealed), §25850 (loaded firearm in public)
various bans on possession by felons/certain misdemeanants; rules on concealed/loaded carry.
examples that count:
• convicted felon found with handgun
• carrying concealed pistol without permit
• loaded rifle in car on public street
• domestic violence restraining order subject with gun
penalty:
mix of misdemeanors/felonies; enhancements possible.
assault weapons / .50 bmg rifles
PC §30600 et seq.
manufacture/sale/transport/possession restrictions with exceptions.
examples that count:
• possessing unregistered AR-15 with banned features
• selling high-capacity magazines
• manufacturing assault weapons without license
penalty:
felonies with significant terms.

drug offenses (health & safety code)

possession of controlled substance
H&S §11350 / §11377 (by substance)
unlawful possession of listed substances.
examples that count:
• having cocaine in pocket during traffic stop
• possessing methamphetamine in backpack
• heroin found in car console
• prescription pills without valid prescription
penalty:
commonly misdemeanor; priors/sales weight/circumstances can elevate.
possession for sale / sales / transport
H&S §11351, §11352, §11359, §11360, §11378, §11379 (varies by drug)
possession with intent to sell; actual sale; transport/distribution.
examples that count:
• large quantities packaged in small baggies
• scales, cash, and drugs found together
• selling drugs to undercover officer
• transporting drugs across county lines
• text messages arranging drug sales
penalty:
typically felonies with multi-year terms; enhancements by weight/locations.

vehicle code (driving)

dui (alcohol/drugs)
VC §23152, §23153 (injury)
driving under influence or 0.08%+ bac; injury version elevates penalties.
examples that count:
• driving with 0.08% or higher blood alcohol
• driving impaired by marijuana, prescription drugs
• causing accident while drunk/high
• failing field sobriety tests due to impairment
penalty:
misdemeanors for first offenses (fines, programs, license actions, possible jail); felonies for injury/multiple priors.
hit & run
VC §20002 (property), §20001 (injury/death)
leaving scene without required info/aid.
examples that count:
• backing into parked car and driving away
• hitting pedestrian and fleeing scene
• side-swiping car on freeway and not stopping
• leaving after injury accident without giving info
penalty:
property = misdemeanor; injury/death can be felony.
reckless driving
VC §23103
willful/wanton disregard for safety.
examples that count:
• racing through residential neighborhood
• weaving through traffic at high speeds
• doing donuts in parking lot with people around
• running red lights repeatedly
penalty:
misdemeanor; higher if injury.
infractions (common)
VC (various)
speeding, stop sign, equipment, phone use, etc. fine-only; not crimes.
examples that count:
• driving 80 mph in 65 mph zone
• rolling through stop sign
• texting while driving
• expired registration or broken tail light
penalty:
fines/points; traffic school eligibility varies.

fraud & forgery

forgery
PC §470
signing another's name or altering/forging certain documents with intent to defraud.
examples that count:
• signing someone else's name on checks
• altering dollar amounts on contracts
• creating fake driver's license
• forging signature on legal documents
penalty:
wobbler; amount/type can affect charge.
embezzlement
PC §503–§515
fraudulent appropriation of property by a person to whom it was entrusted.
examples that count:
• bookkeeper stealing from company accounts
• cashier pocketing register money
• treasurer using club funds for personal expenses
• employee selling company equipment for personal gain
penalty:
tracks petty/grand theft thresholds.
extortion
PC §518–§527
obtaining property or official act via force/fear or under color of official right.
examples that count:
• threatening to release embarrassing photos unless paid
• demanding protection money from businesses
• threatening to harm family unless victim pays
• corrupt official demanding bribes
penalty:
felony (terms vary).

other common offenses

trespass
PC §602
entering/remaining without permission in specified ways/places.
examples that count:
• entering private property after being told to leave
• climbing fence to access restricted area
• remaining in store after closing time
• entering construction site without authorization
penalty:
generally misdemeanor/infraction; facts matter.
arson
PC §451 (willful), §452 (reckless)
maliciously or recklessly setting fire to property/structures/forest land.
examples that count:
• intentionally setting house on fire
• burning down ex-partner's car
• starting wildfire through reckless campfire
• burning business for insurance money
penalty:
felonies; terms vary by damage/injury.
public intoxication
PC §647(f)
in public under the influence to the degree you can't care for safety/obstructing public ways.
examples that count:
• passed out drunk on sidewalk
• stumbling into traffic while intoxicated
• blocking store entrance while heavily drunk
• unable to care for own safety due to intoxication
penalty:
misdemeanor.
prostitution / solicitation
PC §647(b) (note: statutes evolve)
engaging in or soliciting act of prostitution (definitions/defenses nuanced by statute changes).
examples that count:
• offering sex for money
• asking to hire someone for sexual services
• agreeing to exchange money for sexual acts
penalty:
generally misdemeanor; programs/alternatives may apply.
penalty key: "x/y/z years" = statutory triad. wobblers can be filed as misdemeanor or felony.
